## The primary metric: CPA

Cost per acquisition is the number that determines whether your Slopeside test is working. Compare the CPA of your Slopeside ad set against your control ad set over the full 14-day window.

Everything else — CPM, CTR, impressions, reach — is secondary. A test where Slopeside produces higher CPM and lower CPA is a successful test. A test where Slopeside produces lower CPM but the same CPA is inconclusive.

## What the results mean

**Slopeside CPA is lower than control**

Your test suggests Slopeside is working in your account. The size of the difference matters:

- A 10 to 20 percent CPA improvement is a meaningful, commercially significant result — at $10K to $50K monthly spend, that compounds quickly

- A 20 percent or greater improvement is a strong result worth scaling

- A small improvement (under 10 percent) with limited conversion volume may reflect normal variance rather than a real signal — consider extending the test or increasing budget before scaling

**CPAs are similar**

The test is inconclusive. This can happen for a few reasons:

- Not enough conversions on one or both ad sets (fewer than 50 per ad set reduces reliability significantly)

- The test ran through an unusual period — a sale, a product launch, a seasonal spike — that affected one ad set differently

- The audience category may need adjustment — contact support to discuss whether a different Slopeside audience is a better fit for your buyer profile

Do not interpret a similar CPA as evidence that Slopeside does not work. It may mean the test needs more data or a different setup.

**Slopeside CPA is higher than control**

Look at conversion volume first. If either ad set has fewer than 50 conversions, the result is not statistically reliable.

If both ad sets have sufficient conversion volume and Slopeside CPA is still meaningfully higher after 14 days, contact support. There are a few common causes — audience category mismatch, a creative that resonates strongly with your existing audience but not with a new one, or a B2B audience being evaluated over a window that included mostly weekend days.

## Secondary metrics worth reviewing

Once you have confirmed your CPA comparison, these secondary signals add useful context:

| Metric | What it tells you |
|---|---|
| Conversion rate | Are Slopeside clicks converting at a higher rate? Higher conversion rate with similar CPA suggests better traffic quality |
| ROAS | For ecommerce accounts, are Slopeside customers spending more per order? |
| CPM | Expected to be higher on Slopeside — confirms the audience is reaching a more targeted pool |
| CTR | Less meaningful in isolation, but a significantly lower CTR may indicate a creative mismatch with the new audience |

## What to do after a successful test

Scale the Slopeside ad set. Increase budget in increments — no more than 20 to 30 percent every 48 to 72 hours — to avoid triggering a new learning phase.

Test additional audiences. Your trial includes one audience. Paid plans include access to multiple segments. Testing a second or third audience from the catalog helps you identify which behavioral profiles perform best for your specific product and price point.

Use Slopeside audiences as lookalike seeds. Once you have a validated Slopeside audience, it makes an effective seed for Meta's lookalike engine. A lookalike built from an audience of active researchers tends to outperform one built from a static customer list.

## Minimum data thresholds

Do not make scaling or cancellation decisions before your test meets these minimums:

- At least 14 days of runtime

- At least 50 conversions on each ad set

If you have not reached 50 conversions per ad set within 14 days, extend the test or increase the daily budget before drawing conclusions.
